### 2.2 Risk Classification Framework

Every tactic in this report carries an explicit risk label: SAFE, MODERATE, HIGH, or ToS-violating. This section explains how these labels were determined and what they mean for execution.

#### Risk Level Definitions

**SAFE:** The tactic is fully compliant with LinkedIn's Terms of Service, User Agreement, and Professional Community Policies. It carries zero risk of account restriction, ban, or legal liability when executed as described. Examples include manual profile optimization, organic commenting, and native content creation. All Vital Few tactics are SAFE.

**MODERATE:** The tactic is technically compliant but carries a risk of reduced reach, algorithmic suppression, or negative brand perception if executed poorly. It does not violate ToS but may trigger algorithmic penalties if overused or done incorrectly. Examples include single-image posts (30% reach penalty), overposting (algorithmic penalty), and recycled thought leadership (reach suppression). MODERATE tactics are not dangerous to account safety but are suboptimal uses of time and posting opportunities.

**HIGH:** The tactic violates LinkedIn's community norms or best practices and carries a meaningful risk of account restriction, reduced distribution, or reputational damage. While not always explicitly ToS-violating, these tactics signal spammy behavior to the algorithm and can trigger the Volume Tax (throttling to ~30 weekly requests) or soft flags. Examples include mass DM blasts (acceptance rates <30% trigger throttling), purchasing connections/followers (destroys acceptance rates), and maintaining 500+ pending invitations (triggers soft flags). HIGH tactics should be avoided by any account seeking sustainable growth.

**ToS-violating:** The tactic explicitly violates LinkedIn's Terms of Service, User Agreement Section 8.2 (which prohibits automation tools, bots, and scraping), or applicable law (CFAA for unauthorized access). These tactics carry the highest risk: account bans (temporary or permanent), legal action (LinkedIn has actively sued scrapers under the CFAA), and irreversible reputational damage. Examples include browser extension automation, cloud-based automation, fake account creation, scraping, API abuse, engagement pods, and automated commenting. The evidence is clear: even "safe" automation tools face 23% ban rates within 90 days, and browser extensions are actively detected by LinkedIn's scanning systems [SOURCE: Community synthesis

#### Risk Determination Methodology

Risk levels were determined through a structured evaluation process:

1. **ToS Alignment:** Does the tactic violate any explicit clause in LinkedIn's User Agreement, Professional Community Policies, or Cookie Policy? If yes, label as ToS-violating.
2. **Official Documentation:** Does LinkedIn Help or Engineering documentation explicitly warn against or penalize this behavior? If yes, label as HIGH or ToS-violating.
3. **Practitioner Evidence:** Do multiple independent sources (forums, case studies, vendor reports) document account restrictions, bans, or algorithmic penalties for this tactic? If yes, label as HIGH or ToS-violating.
4. **Algorithmic Penalty:** Does the tactic trigger known algorithmic penalties (Volume Tax, reach suppression, spam filtering)? If yes, label as MODERATE or HIGH.
5. **Legal Risk:** Does the tactic create exposure under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA), GDPR, CCPA, or other applicable law? If yes, label as ToS-violating.
